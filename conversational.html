Gulf Briefing
Generated Sunday, March 29, 2026 at 6:24 PM GMT+4 · Refresh

Iran has dramatically escalated its monthlong conflict with the United States by launching direct attacks on multiple Gulf states, marking the first time Iranian forces have struck GCC territory since the war began. Bloomberg reports that Iran targeted Saudi military installations hosting US troops, injuring American personnel, while also hitting industrial facilities in Bahrain and the UAE. The attacks prompted Bahrain to impose a maritime ban from 6 PM to 4 AM daily, according to its Interior Ministry, while the UAE Defense Ministry reported intercepting 16 ballistic missiles and 42 drones in Saturday's barrage alone.

Iran-backed Houthis have formally entered the broader conflict, launching their first missile attack on Israel since the war began, according to AP News. The escalation has revived fears of renewed Red Sea shipping disruptions, with the BBC reporting potential threats to global trade routes. Against this backdrop, Pakistani officials are hosting diplomatic talks with Saudi Arabia, Turkey, and Egypt as regional powers seek to contain the crisis, Reuters reports.

Energy & Economy

• Saudi Arabia's east-west pipeline is now pumping 7 million barrels per day of oil, bypassing the Strait of Hormuz entirely, according to Bloomberg News via Reuters — a critical development as Iranian attacks threaten shipping lanes.

• Most Gulf stock markets declined on fears of broader conflict with Iran, Reuters reports, reflecting investor anxiety about regional stability.

• The UN Secretary-General has formed a special task force to address the humanitarian crisis in the Strait of Hormuz, according to Times of Oman.

• Ukraine's President Zelenskyy is visiting Gulf states seeking long-term fuel supply deals, Bloomberg reports, while also pursuing defense cooperation agreements.

Saudi Arabia

Prince Khalid bin Salman confirmed he discussed Iran's "flagrant attacks on the Kingdom and other regional states" in calls with Hungarian and French defense ministers, posting on X that he condemned the "repeated Iranian attacks targeting the Kingdom." Foreign Minister Prince Faisal bin Farhan is participating in quadripartite ministerial talks in Islamabad with Pakistan, Turkey, and Egypt to address regional developments. Iran's Parliament Speaker Mohammad Ghalibaf rejected a US "15-Point Plan" according to Times of Oman, declaring "we will never be humiliated."

UAE

The UAE Defense Ministry reported intercepting 414 ballistic missiles and 15 cruise missiles since Iranian attacks began, with Saturday alone seeing 16 ballistic missiles and 42 drones engaged. CNN Arabic reports that Iran attacked two aluminum production companies in Bahrain and the UAE. Sheikh Abdullah bin Zayed criticized the UN's "bare minimum" response to the humanitarian crisis, calling for stronger international efforts to end the war.

Qatar

Prime Minister Sheikh Mohammed bin Abdulrahman Al Thani condemned Iranian attacks on Qatar, stating they are "rejected and unjustified under any pretext." Ukraine agreed defense cooperation with both UAE and Qatar during Zelenskyy's Gulf tour, Reuters reports.

Bahrain

Two people were injured in an Iranian attack on Bahrain Aluminum Company, according to CNN Arabic. The Interior Ministry implemented an immediate maritime movement ban from 6 PM to 4 AM "to preserve the safety of sailors and seagoers" following the "blatant Iranian aggression."

Yemen

Houthi forces launched their first missile attack on Israel since the broader Iran conflict began, AP News reports, formally bringing the Iran-backed group into the monthlong war. Mohammed Abdulsalam, the Houthis' chief negotiator, defended naval operations in the Red Sea as supporting Palestinians, while warning that "whoever seeks to expand the conflict must bear the consequences." Saudi Yemen envoy Mohammed Al Jabir posted "#Al-Hudaydah is liberated.....#Yemen is breathing," though the context remains unclear.

Official Signals

Iranian Parliament Speaker Mohammad Ghalibaf's rejection of US diplomatic overtures signals Tehran's unwillingness to negotiate while under military pressure, according to Times of Oman. Oman's Foreign Minister Badr Albusaidi told the Economist that "whatever your view of Iran, this war is not of their making," positioning Muscat as a potential mediator while maintaining its policy of constructive neutrality.
